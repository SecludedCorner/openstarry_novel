# Saṅkhāra-skandha Research Reference Document

> This document is consolidated from multiple discussions, for use as reference by the research team.
> Research direction: exploring the meaning and architectural value of Saṅkhāra-skandha (the aggregate of volitional formations) through original scriptures (Evaṃ me sutaṃ — "Thus have I heard").

---

## I. Conditions for Using Buddhist Terminology

The use of Buddhist terminology and Sanskrit must simultaneously satisfy both of the following conditions:

1. **Systematic planning** — there is a complete architectural design, not scattered decoration
2. **Meaning fully corresponds** — the engineering function genuinely matches the Buddhist definition

Mappings that do not satisfy the above conditions should revert to engineering language and not have Buddhist labels forced onto them.

## II. The Nature of the Five Aggregates in Original Scriptures

In the scriptures prefaced by "Evaṃ me sutaṃ" (Thus have I heard), the Buddha's purpose in teaching the Five Aggregates (Pañca-skandha) was to "break the illusion of self" (anātman), not to establish a rigorous psychological classification model.

The descriptions in the sūtras are observations of phenomena:

> "Form (rūpa) is like a lump of foam, feeling (vedanā) like a water bubble, perception (saññā) like a mirage, volitional formations (saṅkhāra) like a plantain trunk, consciousness (viññāṇa) like a magic show."

The Five Aggregates are five waves arising simultaneously, intertwined with one another, with no clear administrative ownership. This quality is entirely consistent with OpenStarry's co-arising model (MN 43) — in that instant of system operation, the Five Aggregates form a tightly coupled whole, not modules that can be individually separated.

## III. The Original Appearance of "Saṅkhāra" in the Scriptures

In the original scriptures, "Saṅkhāra" (formations / volitional activities) typically refers directly to:

- **Kāya-saṅkhāra, vacī-saṅkhāra, citta-saṅkhāra** (bodily, verbal, and mental formations — the three formations)
- **Cha cetanā-kāya** (the six groups of volition corresponding to the six sense bases)

These are concrete intentions and volitional acts, not the "catch-all of mental factors (caitta)" that later commentators compiled.

The claim that mental factors such as "faith (saddhā), effort (vīriya), desire (lobha)" all belong to Saṅkhāra-skandha is a later commentarial interpretation — produced in order to fit all psychological phenomena into the Five Aggregates framework — and is not the original intent of the scriptures. The Yogācāra school likely did this following the logic that "anything possessing the quality of mental activity and transformation should be classified under formations."

**But this does not mean the scope of Saṅkhāra-skandha stops here.** These mental factors do not necessarily lack qualities of other aggregates, such as components of Saññā-skandha (perception) and Vijñāna-skandha (consciousness). The classifications in the treatises (abhidharma/śāstra) are one interpretive choice, not the final boundary.

## IV. The Original Architecture of Nāma-Rūpa-Vijñāna

In the early scriptures, the Buddha often divided the world into "Nāma" (name/mentality) and "Rūpa" (form/materiality):

| Category | Content |
|----------|---------|
| **Rūpa** | Materiality |
| **Nāma** | Vedanā (feeling), saññā (perception), saṅkhāra (formations), cetanā (volition), phassa (contact), manasikāra (attention) |
| **Vijñāna** | Consciousness — awareness existing independently of nāma |

In this architecture, vedanā, saññā, saṅkhāra, and cetanā are placed side by side under "Nāma," all serving as objects observed by consciousness.

Later, the Five Aggregates model extracted vedanā and saññā into independent aggregates to emphasize their crucial impact on practice, which caused the definition of Saṅkhāra-skandha to become unclear and even overlap with the functions of vijñāna. This is a gap between the original teaching method (contextual/situational teaching) and the later Abhidharma (systematic treatises), not a contradiction within the original scriptures themselves.

## V. The Position of Keeping Saṅkhāra-skandha Open

Master has ruled: **Saṅkhāra-skandha remains open.**

Engineering implications:

- Sub-interfaces of `ISamskara` should not presume exhaustive enumeration
- "Activity and transformation" can serve as one observational angle for exploring subcategories, but not as a boundary definition
- The scope of Saṅkhāra-skandha can continue to expand as research deepens, leaving room for future plugins to enter
- This fully aligns with the spirit of the core declarations: "everything is a plugin" and "replaceability"

---

## VI. Suggested Research Directions

Return to original scriptures; do not take treatise-based classifications as the default answer. Specific suggestions:

**Priority reference scriptures**
- Saṅkhāra-saṃyutta (Connected Discourses on Formations, in the Saṃyutta Nikāya)
- Descriptions of the three formations (kāya-saṅkhāra, vacī-saṅkhāra, citta-saṅkhāra) in the Saṃyuktāgama
- The role of "saṅkhāra" as a link in the Twelve Links of Dependent Origination (Dvādaśa-nidāna) — the dynamic relationship of "formations condition consciousness" (saṅkhāra-paccayā viññāṇaṃ)
- The co-arising description of saṅkhāra with other aggregates in the Mahāvedalla Sutta (MN 43, Majjhima Nikāya)

**Core research questions**

1. Starting from the "three formations," which engineering functions naturally correspond to bodily, verbal, and mental formations? How can these correspondences be translated into sub-interface designs?
2. How does the boundary between Saṅkhāra-skandha and Vijñāna-skandha appear in the original scriptures? Which states possess qualities of both, and should be designed as cross-aggregate plugins?
3. What aspects of Saṅkhāra-skandha can the "activity and transformation" observational angle bring out that are obscured by treatise-based classifications?
4. What insights does the dependent-origination role of "saṅkhāra" in the Twelve Links offer for the execution loop design of agent core?

**How to submit research findings**

Each finding must be cross-referenced with specific scriptural evidence, propose corresponding engineering significance and possible sub-interface directions, and be submitted to Master for ruling before entering architectural design.
