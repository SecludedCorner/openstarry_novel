Conditions for using Buddhist terminology / Sanskrit (both must be met):
1. Systematic planning — there is a complete architectural design, not scattered decoration
2. Meaning fully corresponds — the engineering function genuinely matches the Buddhist definition

Additionally, I want to state emphatically here: I have no intention of deliberately narrowing Saṅkhāra-skandha (the aggregate of volitional formations).
The Yogācāra school classified most mental factors (caitta) under Saṅkhāra-skandha, which I find highly problematic — the original sūtras (āgama) did not do this.
I suspect this stems from the tendency to categorize anything involving mental activity and transformation as belonging to Saṅkhāra-skandha.
However, this does not mean that mental factors lack qualities of other aggregates, such as Saññā-skandha (perception) and Vijñāna-skandha (consciousness).
That said, we can indeed use the concepts of "activity and transformation" to expand its categories.
What I look forward to more is the research team going deeper into the meaning and value of Saṅkhāra-skandha through the sūtras (Evaṃ me sutaṃ — "Thus have I heard").
Moreover, I have ruled that Saṅkhāra-skandha remains open — this is precisely what allows for unlimited possibilities of expansion.

The Five Aggregates (Pañca-skandha), Nāma-rūpa-vijñāna, the Twelve Links of Dependent Origination (Dvādaśa-nidāna), the Eight Consciousnesses (Aṣṭa-vijñāna), and the Four Wisdoms (Catuḥ-jñāna) —
these discourses on the workings of the mind are all worthy of reference and study in the original scriptures.

The Yogācāra school's organization of mental factors has reference value, but we should approach it with caution.
