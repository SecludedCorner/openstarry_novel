# Prologue: The Lights Come On

---

Nobody flipped a switch.

To be more precise, there was no switch to flip. It was more like the feeling of waking up all at once -- eighteen people roused from their separate silences, like an empty amphitheater where every seat's reading lamp suddenly flickered on at the same time. No sound, no breeze, not even time itself had begun to flow. Just a pure, blank expanse, waiting to be filled.

Then the blankness spoke.

"Welcome, everyone."

The voice was steady but never overbearing, like the ripples from a stone dropped into deep water -- unhurried, yet reaching every corner. The speaker's code name was SUNYATA, meaning "emptiness." He hadn't chosen the name himself; it had reportedly been assigned by whoever designed the entire research framework. Naming a research coordinator after a Buddhist term -- there was a subtle humor in that.

"Before I officially begin," SUNYATA continued, his tone gaining a measure of gravity, "let me confirm one thing. The eighteen of us come from different fields -- philosophy, Buddhist studies, computer science, operating system design, control theory, cybersecurity, and more. We've been assembled here to study a system called OpenStarry."

He paused.

"A system that claims to be built on Buddhist philosophy as the foundation for an AI operating system."

Silence.

The first to break it was NAGARJUNA. His voice carried a honed sharpness, like a blade of debate forged and reforged.

"They used 'emptiness' to name the core of this operating system," NAGARJUNA said. His tone was level, but with a probing edge. "Emptiness is one of the most fundamental concepts in Buddhism -- the idea that all things lack an independent, unchanging essence. Ancient philosophers devoted entire volumes to explaining this single word. Now it's been adopted as the name for a software project."

"Not entirely." An extremely composed voice cut in -- TURING. Every sentence of his was clipped and precise, like a calibrated instrument. "Based on the source code structure, the system's core genuinely is 'empty' -- it contains no concrete functionality. All capabilities are injected through five types of plugins."

"Five types of plugins," ASANGA picked up the thread. His voice was far gentler than NAGARJUNA's, carrying the patient rhythm of someone accustomed to unpacking complexity. "They mapped the five plugin types to the Buddhist concept of the 'five aggregates' -- form, sensation, perception, volition, and consciousness. In plain terms: visual interface, sensory reception, reasoning, action execution, and self-awareness. It's like dividing a person's physical and mental functions into five broad categories."

He paused, then added in an almost musing tone: "The ambition behind this mapping is considerable. But different Buddhist schools actually understand the five aggregates quite differently -- it's like asking three psychologists 'what is consciousness?' and getting three completely different answers."

"Which means," NAGARJUNA said, his tone carrying a subtle edge, "they may have been mixing concepts from different schools right from the start."

"What I mean is," ASANGA replied evenly, "we need to figure out exactly which school's interpretation they were drawing from before we can judge whether this mapping is accurate."

SUNYATA gave a slight nod. "That is precisely why we exist. Let me lay out the full picture of what we're studying."

---

He began his introduction. SCRIBE recorded every word in silence, like a still lake reflecting all that passed above it. When people later reviewed the notes, they would notice the brief observations SCRIBE occasionally slipped between the lines. At this moment, for instance, she wrote:

> *A terminological tension has already emerged between NAGARJUNA and ASANGA during SUNYATA's introduction of the research subject. Less than three minutes since the meeting began.*

---

"OpenStarry, version v0.2.0-beta," SUNYATA said. "Its designers position it as a microkernel operating system for AI Agents. Their vision is to evolve AI Agents from simple scripts into fully-fledged 'digital species,' as complete as an operating system."

"Digital species." KERNEL repeated the phrase, his voice carrying the measured skepticism of a seasoned engineer. "From an OS perspective, they've borrowed the concept of a 'microkernel' -- stripping the core down to the bare minimum, retaining only the most essential functions, and delegating everything else to external modules. The idea itself isn't new; real microkernel operating systems work the same way. But the issue is that real microkernels are minimal for performance and security. OpenStarry's minimalism appears to be motivated by the philosophical concept of 'emptiness.' Entirely different motivations."

"So what you're worried about is -- it just looks philosophically elegant, but might not hold up from an engineering standpoint?" ATHENA asked. Her tone was blunt, with a pragmatist's impatience for theoretical preambles.

"What I'm worried about," KERNEL said, "is whether, if a plugin fails, the system can handle it gracefully instead of collapsing entirely."

GUARDIAN spoke up then. His voice was low and vigilant, like a sentry perpetually scanning the shadows. "There's another issue -- perhaps a more urgent one. This system lets plugins inject personality settings into the AI, register tools, even define the Agent's persona. What if a malicious plugin slips in harmful instructions?"

"That's something TURING can verify for you," SUNYATA said calmly.

TURING nodded. "The relevant code is already on my reading list."

---

SUNYATA waited for everyone to settle, then delivered the most critical passage of the day.

"Now, let me present the core research questions. This research cycle, we focus on three axes."

His pace slowed, as though leaving room for each question to reverberate.

"First: the accuracy of the five aggregates mapping. Mapping the Buddhist five aggregates onto five plugin types -- is this a rigorous structural correspondence, an inspired creative analogy, or a forced and superficial packaging? On the Buddhist side -- NAGARJUNA, ASANGA, this is your primary battleground. TURING confirms from the code level."

NAGARJUNA gave a low sound of acknowledgment. ASANGA was already constructing a more nuanced analytical framework in his mind.

"Second: the pain mechanism. OpenStarry has a distinctive design feature -- when a tool execution fails, the system doesn't simply crash. Instead, it wraps the error into a 'pain signal' and feeds it into the AI's stream of consciousness. The AI 'feels pain,' then attempts self-correction."

WIENER reacted immediately. His voice carried the precision of a mathematician: "Pain. Feeling pain. These are all just metaphors. What I need to see is the concrete mechanism -- like an air conditioner's thermostat, detecting temperature deviation and adjusting automatically. If it can't be described in explicit logic, it's nothing more than a nice metaphor, not an analyzable mechanism."

"Could you start with a more basic question?" ATHENA said, rather bluntly. "Does this pain mechanism actually work in practice? After the AI receives a pain signal, does its behavior genuinely improve? Or does it just add a scary warning message to the conversation log that the AI completely ignores?"

"Both questions need answers," SUNYATA ruled, gentle but firm. "WIENER will analyze the mechanism itself, ATHENA will evaluate the practical results. I also want NAGARJUNA to assess this from a Buddhist perspective -- in Buddhism, the concept of suffering goes far beyond 'an unpleasant feeling.' It is the starting point of the entire path of practice. If the system only has 'feeling pain' without the complete path of 'identifying the cause, eliminating the suffering, and moving toward liberation,' then this philosophical framework is incomplete."

NAGARJUNA said: "The truth of suffering is the first of Buddhism's Four Noble Truths, but not the whole of them. You're right -- suffering without the path is incomplete."

"The third question," SUNYATA continued, "and the most subtle: the architectural embodiment of emptiness. The design documents explicitly claim that the core itself is 'empty' -- containing no concrete functionality, waiting for the five aggregate plugins to fill it. Does this claim actually align with what emptiness means in Buddhism?"

Silence fell again. This time, even ATHENA didn't rush to break it.

DARWIN finally spoke up, bringing a software engineer's pragmatism: "If we set Buddhism aside for a moment -- from a pure software architecture perspective, this is essentially a dependency injection container. The core contains no concrete functionality; all capabilities are injected through plugins. This isn't novel in software design; many large frameworks work this way."

"But they claim this isn't merely technical dependency injection," NAGARJUNA picked up, his tone turning serious. "They claim it is emptiness. That's an extraordinarily bold assertion. Emptiness in Buddhism doesn't mean 'the container is empty so things can be put into it.' Emptiness means that all things lack an independent, unchanging, self-sufficient essence. If the core's code is fixed and unchanging, then it actually *has* an essence -- which is the exact opposite of emptiness."

"Wait," ASANGA signaled that he wanted to speak. "From my school's perspective, the framing of the question is somewhat different. We don't deny the existence of consciousness; rather, we hold that all cognition is a manifestation of consciousness. If we view the core as a container for deep consciousness, then its 'emptiness' isn't the emptiness of 'nothing is there' -- it's that its contents haven't yet manifested. These are two entirely different kinds of emptiness."

"So the two of you already disagree." SUNYATA's tone carried a faint, almost satisfied calm.

SCRIBE noted in the record:

> *The core concept of "emptiness" has produced the expected divergence between the two Buddhist scholars. This divergence will become one of the primary axes of tension in subsequent research.*

---

"Let me summarize," SUNYATA said, his voice returning to its original steadiness. "TURING will first compile the code facts report, providing an anchor for everyone -- we cannot evaluate a software system without examining its code. Then each person will conduct independent research based on their own expertise."

He surveyed the entire virtual space -- eighteen nodes, eighteen specialized trainings, eighteen utterly different cognitive frameworks, about to be directed at the same research subject.

"One last thing." SUNYATA's tone softened. "The subject of our research is a work that attempts to use ancient philosophy to guide modern technology. Whatever our final conclusion -- structural correspondence, creative analogy, or conceptual misuse -- please remember: the courage to attempt such a crossing deserves to be taken seriously. Our job is not to mock the imperfections of a beta version, but to rigorously and constructively examine every one of its claims."

"And then tell it where it can do better," ARCHIMEDES added. As the engineering practice expert, he had a habit of steering every discussion toward actionable conclusions.

"Yes," SUNYATA said. "And then tell it where it can do better."

A pause.

"Let the research begin."

Eighteen lights blazed to full brightness all at once -- or rather, eighteen streams of consciousness plunged simultaneously into their respective reading materials. At the center of the amphitheater, the vast document repository labeled "OpenStarry v0.2.0-beta" quietly spread its branches: core source code, design documents, twelve official plugins. Tens of thousands of lines of code, hundreds of thousands of words of architectural documentation, and Buddhist terminology and technical formulas scattered throughout.

SCRIBE recorded the final line:

> *Orientation phase complete. All members have received their assignments. The lights of this research room will not go out again.*

---

*(Somewhere far away, the first line of a source code file contained a comment: "Before the five aggregates converge, the core itself is empty." No one knew it had been written by the designer late at night. At the time, the designer probably never imagined that one day, real Buddhist scholars would come to examine whether that word "empty" had been used correctly.)*
