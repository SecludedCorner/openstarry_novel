# 第四章：五遍行 — Chapter 4: The Five Universals

---

ASANGA did something unusual before the debate began.

He closed his eyes.

Not meditation — he would not formally meditate in the amphitheater; that was time for personal practice, not academic debate. The way he closed his eyes was more like a person opening a drawer deep in memory: first letting the external light exit, then letting the internal text surface.

The scripture he was about to cite had dwelt in his mind for fifteen hundred years. Not his own fifteen hundred years — but the fifteen hundred years during which those words had been recited, copied, translated, debated. Each citation was catching a thread thrown from the hand of Bodhisattva Asanga.

He opened his eyes.

"In the first debate we established the dual-layer dual-gear architecture. CoarisingBundle computes atomically at the speed of vedana-clock." His voice was steady, like a great stone on a riverbed. "But that debate left one question unanswered."

He looked toward SUNYATA.

"What exactly goes inside the Bundle."

---

> *SCRIBE aside: The core conflict of Debate 2 can be summarized by a single number: 3 vs 5. The R01 research report defined three CoarisingBundle components — vedana, samjna, and cetana. R03 proposed five — adding sparsha (contact) and manasikara (attention), in full accordance with the Yogacara doctrine of the five universals (panca sarvatraga). A difference of two. Two elements sounds like not much. But in a sub-millisecond computation bundle, every element has a cost. The question is not "what do the scriptures say" — the question is "can engineering afford what the scriptures say."*

---

SUNYATA stood at the center of the theater.

"Debate 2. The composition of CoarisingBundle. Three universals, or five universals."

His gaze swept across the amphitheater. On the left, ASANGA and NAGARJUNA sat side by side, separated by the distance of one seat — enough to let two Buddhist traditions each breathe. On the right, KERNEL's fingers were already flipping through his analogy cards. In the back row, PASCAL's notebook lay open on his knees, a draft Bayesian calculation table occupying the entire page.

"ASANGA. Doctrinal argument first."

## I

ASANGA stood up.

The way he rose had not changed across three debates — like a mountain revealing its contours in morning mist. Slow. Impossible to ignore. But today he held something extra: a sheet of paper. Not a printed academic paper, but handwritten — Sanskrit verses he had copied himself in blue fountain pen, the handwriting as neat as that of a sutra-copying scribe in a monastery.

He held up the sheet of paper so the entire audience could see it.

"The five universals." he said. "*Panca sarvatraga*. Sanskrit *sarva* means 'all,' *traga* derives from the verb *tṛ*, meaning 'to go, to reach, to pervade.' Sarvatraga — reaching all places. These five mental factors accompany every cognitive moment (*citta-kṣaṇa*), without exception."

He walked to the whiteboard and wrote five names in black marker. With each one he paused for half a second — not hesitation, but solemnity. Giving each name that had been written for fifteen hundred years its proper weight.

```
Five Universals (Pañca Sarvatraga)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════
 1. Contact    sparśa     — union of root, object, and consciousness
 2. Feeling    vedanā     — threefold reception: pain, pleasure, neutral
 3. Perception saṃjñā     — grasping characteristics, discrimination
 4. Volition   cetanā     — constructing force, driving action
 5. Attention  manasikāra — mental alertness, directing toward the object
═══════════════════════════════════════════════
```

"I will cite two foundational texts." He placed the handwritten sheet beside the whiteboard. "The first is Vasubandhu's *Pañcaskandhaprakaraṇa* — one of the foundational texts of Yogacara."

His voice slowed slightly when citing scripture — not deliberate solemnity, but a respect for the precision of the original text. Every word was placed in its proper position, like the final pieces of a puzzle.

> "云何觸？謂三和合分別為性。三和合者，謂根境識更相會也。"
> — Vasubandhu, *Pañcaskandhaprakaraṇa*

"Contact. *Sparśa*. Not physical touch — it is the union of root (indriya), object (viṣaya), and consciousness (vijñāna). In OpenStarry's language, this is SparshEvent: the IListener channel (root) receives an InputEvent (object), meeting in the session context (consciousness). Contact is the instant these three converge."

His finger moved from contact to attention.

"The second foundational text. The *Abhidharmasamuccaya* — the treatise of Bodhisattva Asanga. The source of my name."

> "云何作意？謂令心發悟為性。於所緣境引心趨向為業。"
> — Asanga, *Abhidharmasamuccaya*

"Attention. *Manasikāra*. Literally 'making the mind (manas) function (kāra).' Its function is 'causing the mind to awaken' — rousing the mind from distraction to alertness — and then 'directing the mind toward the object of cognition' — guiding attention toward a specific object."

He paused for a second. Then he said the first sharp-edged sentence of this debate.

"R01's CoarisingBundle has only three elements: vedana, samjna, cetana. Contact and attention are entirely missing."

He looked at the entire audience.

"A cognitive moment lacking any one of the universals is not a cognitive moment. In the strict Abhidharma definition, the five universals accompany all mental states, without exception. This is definitional, not empirical. Just as you cannot have a triangle with only two sides — not because you have never seen one, but because the definition of a triangle requires three sides. The simultaneous presence of the five universals is a definitional requirement of a cognitive moment."

He drew a circle around the five names with his finger.

"All five are indispensable."

---

NAGARJUNA spoke the instant ASANGA finished drawing that circle.

He did not stand up. The Madhyamaka philosopher never stands when issuing a warning — the force of a warning lies not in posture but in acuity. His voice rose from his seat, sharp but not shrill, like a stone polished by a thousand years of river water.

"ASANGA, you are reifying the mental-factor classification system."

The air in the theater tightened.

"You say 'all five are indispensable.' You cite the *Pañcaskandhaprakaraṇa* and the *Abhidharmasamuccaya*. These are great treatises. But what is the function of a treatise? The function of a treatise is teaching (*deśanā*) — helping practitioners understand how the mind works. A treatise is not an ontological inventory."

He paused for a beat. Then expanded with more precise language.

"The five universals are a **pedagogical tool** (*upāya*). Not an ontological declaration. They are one systematic description of how mental factors operate — a particularly precise, particularly useful description — but the description is not the thing itself. The map is not the terrain."

> "若人見般若，是則為被縛；若不見般若，是亦為被縛。"
> — Nagarjuna, *Mulamadhyamakakarika*, Chapter XXV: Examination of Nirvana

"If we include five because the treatise says five, we are doing Buddhist archaeology, not engineering."

He looked toward ASANGA. Not confrontation — correction. Like two mirrors illuminating each other's blind spots.

"My position is not 'do not include five.' My position is: if we include five, the reason must be functional, not doctrinal. Functional arguments are more reliable than doctrinal arguments. Because functional arguments can be verified by engineering. Doctrinal arguments can only be endorsed by authority."

---

> *SCRIBE aside: The divergence between NAGARJUNA and ASANGA had appeared as early as Cycle 02-2. In Chapter Three, "Parts and Wholes," NAGARJUNA applied the tetralemma negation to the naming choice for IVijnana — not because he denied the complexity of vijnana-skandha, but because he insisted that a name must be "the conventional designation least likely to cause misunderstanding." Now, in Debate 2, the same methodological divergence resurfaces: ASANGA starts from doctrine, NAGARJUNA demands functional justification.*

> *But note — NAGARJUNA did not say "do not include five." He said "the justification must be functional." This is a methodological challenge, not a negation of the conclusion. He is controlling the quality of the argument, not the direction of the argument.*

## II

KERNEL stood up after NAGARJUNA finished.

He held three cards in his hand — those analogy cards with operating system concepts on the left and OpenStarry mappings on the right. But this time, he did not flip through them. He walked to the side of the whiteboard and drew a timing diagram below ASANGA's five-universals list.

"Let me ask an engineering question." he said. His voice low and steady, like a daemon running continuously in kernel space. "I am not questioning the Buddhism. I am questioning the timing."

He wrote two rows of numbers on the whiteboard:

```
Layer 1 computation budget: < 1ms
Estimated cost of each element in the five universals:
  sparsha  (contact)    — SparshEvent construction   ~0.01ms  ✓
  vedana   (feeling)    — ChannelVedana computation  ~0.1ms   ✓
  samjna   (perception) — ChannelSamjna matching     ~0.05ms  ✓
  cetana   (volition)   — ChannelCetana judgment     ~0.05ms  ✓
  manasikara (attention) — ???                       ???      ?
```

"The first four are fine." He tapped the checkmarks on the first four rows. "SparshEvent is a struct construction — three readonly fields, virtually zero cost. ChannelVedana is a numerical mapping — valence and intensity computed directly from sensor readings. ChannelSamjna is rule matching — in VasanaEngine's fast path, this is a hash table lookup. ChannelCetana is an approach-avoidance judgment — derived directly from the sign of valence."

His finger stopped at the fifth row.

"Attention. *Manasikāra*. The direction of attention."

He turned to ASANGA.

"ASANGA, you said the function of manasikara is 'directing the mind toward the object of cognition.' In OpenStarry's architecture, who determines the direction of attention?"

ASANGA answered: "IGuide. The system prompt defines what the Agent should attend to."

KERNEL nodded. "IGuide. In Cycle 02-2, IGuide was classified under vijnana-skandha — a sub-interface of IVijnana. Its primary method is `getSystemPrompt()`. And the invocation of `getSystemPrompt()` —"

He drew a timeline on the whiteboard:

```
Clock domain analysis: IGuide.getSystemPrompt()
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  IGuide belongs to vijnana-clock (1~5ms)
  getSystemPrompt() involves:
    - Reading system prompt configuration          ~0.1ms
    - Context assembly                             ~0.5ms
    - Possible dynamic prompt generation           ~1-5ms
  ─────────────────────────────────────
  Total: 1~5ms, a vijnana-clock operation

  Layer 1 belongs to vedana-clock (10~100ms per tick, <1ms computation)
```

"Layer 1 must complete within 1ms." KERNEL's voice grew heavier — not emotional emphasis, but the emphasis of engineering constraint. "If manasikara requires calling `IGuide.getSystemPrompt()`, that is a vijnana-clock operation — 1 to 5 milliseconds. You cannot place a 5ms operation inside a sub-millisecond bundle."

He wrote a large X after the question marks.

"If manasikara = IGuide.getSystemPrompt(), the cost is 1~5ms. Exceeds Layer 1's budget. Not feasible."

He looked at the entire audience.

"This is the engineering core of the three-element vs five-element debate. Not a question of whether Buddhism is right or wrong. A question of whether there is enough time."

---

ARCHIMEDES stood up immediately after KERNEL wrote that large X.

The speed at which he rose was faster than usual — not eagerness, but the kind of speed that comes from "an engineering solution has already formed and cannot wait to come out." He walked to KERNEL's timing diagram and picked up a green marker — green, in his marking system, meant "solution."

"KERNEL, your analysis has an implicit assumption." he said. His voice steady as brick, every word laid in its correct position.

He drew an arrow beside KERNEL's large X, pointing to a new box:

```
KERNEL's assumption: manasikara = IGuide.getSystemPrompt()
                                  ↓
                            Dynamic computation (1~5ms)

ARCHIMEDES' alternative: manasikara = snapshot
                                  ↓
                            Read precomputed result (~0.01ms)
```

"ChannelManasikara is not computation. ChannelManasikara is a **snapshot**."

He said the word with weight. Not tonal emphasis — engineering-semantic emphasis. In concurrent system design, snapshot and computation are two entirely different categories of operation.

"Let me explain how the snapshot pattern works."

He drew a cross-clock-domain data flow diagram on the whiteboard:

```
Cross-clock-domain snapshot mechanism
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  vijnana-clock (1~5ms)              vedana-clock (10~100ms)
  ┌──────────────────┐              ┌──────────────────────┐
  │ IGuide           │  write       │ CoarisingBundle      │
  │  .getSystemPrompt│──────→ ┌───┐ │  .manasikara         │
  │  .computeAttention│       │snap│←──── read              │
  │                  │──────→ │shot│ │    (readonly access)  │
  │ Precompute       │       │mem │ │                      │
  │ attention state  │       │    │ │                      │
  └──────────────────┘       └───┘ └──────────────────────┘
                                ↑
                           SharedAttentionState
                           (lock-free atomic read)

  Write cost: borne by vijnana-clock (does not consume Layer 1 budget)
  Read cost: ~0.01ms (single atomic read, negligible)
```

"vijnana-clock handles the computation. It runs IGuide's logic in its own clock domain — reads the system prompt, analyzes context, determines attention direction — then writes the result into shared memory. vedana-clock's Layer 1 does only one thing: reads the latest snapshot from that shared memory. One atomic read. 0.01 milliseconds."

He turned to KERNEL.

"The 5ms cost you are worried about is the cost of computation. The 0.01ms I am talking about is the cost of reading. ChannelManasikara does not compute — it reads the result of computation. Just as you would not compute an FFT inside an interrupt handler — you compute it in a background thread, then read the result in the ISR."

He wrote a TypeScript interface on the whiteboard:

```typescript
/**
 * ChannelManasikara — channel-level snapshot of attention.
 *
 * Not a computation — a readonly read of vijnana-clock's precomputed result.
 * Cost: ~0.01ms (atomic read from SharedAttentionState).
 *
 * @sanskrit manasikāra — "causing the mind to awaken, directing it toward the object"
 * @clock-domain vedana-clock (reader) / vijnana-clock (writer)
 */
interface ChannelManasikara {
  readonly focus: string;       // what this channel is attending to
  readonly intensity: number;   // attention intensity (0.0 ~ 1.0)
  readonly source: 'guide' | 'context' | 'default';
  readonly stale: boolean;      // whether the snapshot has expired
}
```

"Four fields. All `readonly`. `focus` indicates the direction of attention — what this channel is currently attending to. `intensity` is the strength of attention — 0.0 means completely unattended, 1.0 means maximum alertness. `source` marks the origin of the snapshot — from IGuide's configuration, from contextual inference, or using a default value. `stale` marks whether the snapshot has expired — if the last update from vijnana-clock exceeds the staleness bound, this flag is true."

He stepped back.

"Engineering cost: 0.01 milliseconds. Well within Layer 1's 1ms budget."

He looked at the large X on KERNEL's whiteboard.

"You can replace that X with a checkmark now."

---

KERNEL looked at ARCHIMEDES' cross-clock-domain diagram for a full five seconds.

In the operating system expert's world, cross-clock-domain data sharing is a problem he deals with every day. Cache coherence between CPU cores. MMIO (Memory-Mapped I/O) between PCI Express devices and the host. Interrupt status registers between the interrupt controller and the CPU. All of these scenarios follow the same pattern: one writer updates state in its own clock domain, another reader reads the latest value in its own clock domain.

"Lock-free atomic read." he said. No objection. Only confirmation. "On x86, an aligned 64-bit read is inherently atomic. No lock required. No CAS (Compare-And-Swap) required. As long as the shared memory address is 8-byte aligned, the read operation is naturally atomic."

He added a small line of text beside ARCHIMEDES' diagram:

```
Hardware guarantee: x86 aligned 64-bit load is atomic (Intel SDM Vol. 3A, §8.1.1)
TypeScript equivalent: SharedArrayBuffer + Atomics.load()
Actual cost: single memory read, ~10ns = 0.00001ms
```

"0.01ms is a conservative estimate." he said. "The actual cost is on the nanosecond scale."

He picked up that card — the one whose right side had been blank — and wrote a line on the right side:

Left: `MMIO snapshot read (cross-clock domain)`
Right: `ChannelManasikara (vijnana→vedana snapshot)`

He erased the large X. Replaced it with a checkmark.

---

> *SCRIBE aside: ARCHIMEDES' snapshot solution shifted the center of gravity of the entire debate. Before his contribution, the axis of the debate was "Buddhist doctrine vs engineering budget" — ASANGA on the doctrine side, KERNEL on the budget side. After his contribution, the axis disappeared. Not because one side won — but because a third path emerged: the snapshot. Computation costs are borne by vijnana-clock; Layer 1 bears only the read cost. Doctrinal requirements and engineering constraints are no longer in conflict.*

> *I noticed that KERNEL's motion of erasing the large X took only one second. In the OS expert's mental model, once hardware facts are confirmed (the cost of an aligned atomic read), the conclusion is determined. No emotional resistance. Only factual update.*

## III

PASCAL stood up after ARCHIMEDES' snapshot proposal was confirmed by KERNEL.

He was a new member who joined in Cycle 02-3 — a specialist in decision theory and the philosophy of probability. His arrival expanded the research team from 19 to 20 members. But his voice had already established its own position across the first three debates: whenever a debate became mired in qualitative judgments of "should we or shouldn't we," he would stand up and transform the question into a quantitative calculation of "what is the expected utility."

"Let me shift the debate framework from qualitative to quantitative." he said. His voice carried a calm peculiar to probabilists — not indifference, but the discipline of "calculate all possible outcomes before making a decision."

He wrote two options in his notebook, then held it up for the entire audience to see:

```
Decision matrix
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
                        manasikara needed     manasikara not needed
                        (P = p)               (P = 1-p)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Include manasikara      Cost: 0.01ms          Cost: 0.01ms
(Action A: include)     Benefit: consistency V Benefit: 0 (waste)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Exclude manasikara      Cost: 0               Cost: 0
(Action B: exclude)     Loss: inconsistency -L Benefit: 0 (correct)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

"Two action options: include or exclude. Two states of nature: downstream systems need manasikara information, or they do not. Four quadrants."

He began computing expected utilities. His writing unfolded on the whiteboard like a set of precision gears meshing together:

$$EU(\text{include}) = p \cdot V + (1-p) \cdot (-0.01\text{ms})$$

$$EU(\text{exclude}) = p \cdot (-L) + (1-p) \cdot 0$$

"$V$ is the value of consistency — CoarisingBundle's consumers (ManoAggregator, VasanaEngine, VitakkaEngine, IVolition) can know the attention direction of each bundle. $L$ is the loss from inconsistency — downstream consumers need attention information but cannot find it."

He paused for a second, then said the sharpest sentence of this debate.

"What is the upper bound of $L$?"

He looked toward KERNEL.

"If ManoAggregator, when aggregating bundles from multiple root-gates, does not know the attention direction of each bundle — does not know which channel the Agent is currently attending to — how does it determine weights? The answer is: it cannot. It either assigns equal weights (losing attention information), assigns random weights (introducing uncertainty), or hardcodes weights (losing adaptability). Three degradation scenarios, each of which propagates downstream."

He drew a causal arrow chain on the whiteboard:

```
manasikara missing
  → ManoAggregator weight degradation
    → VasanaEngine matching quality decline
      → Gear 1/Gear 2 switching judgment inaccuracy
        → Agent behavior quality degradation
          → ??? (unbounded)
```

"Loss propagates along the causal chain. And the length of the causal chain determines the amplification factor of the loss. Each layer can amplify upstream errors. In the worst case — if VasanaEngine switches to Gear 2 when it should not due to missing attention information — the loss is one unnecessary LLM call: 500ms to 30 seconds. If VasanaEngine fails to switch to Gear 2 when it should due to missing attention information — the loss is one erroneous automated decision. The upper bound of this loss is indeterminate."

He added an upward arrow and a question mark beside $L$: $L \to ?$

"This is the engineering version of Pascal's Wager." He put down the notebook and looked directly at the entire audience. "Blaise Pascal, in his 1670 *Pensees*, proposed an argument: if God exists, the benefit of belief is infinite; if God does not exist, the cost of belief is finite. Therefore, even if the probability of God's existence is small, the expected utility calculation supports belief."

"I am not making a theological argument." he added — NAGARJUNA had already furrowed his brow slightly. "I am performing a risk analysis. The structure is identical:"

$$EU(\text{include}) = -0.01\text{ms} + p \cdot V$$
$$EU(\text{exclude}) = 0 + p \cdot (-L)$$

$$EU(\text{include}) - EU(\text{exclude}) = -0.01\text{ms} + p \cdot (V + L)$$

"As long as $p \cdot (V + L) > 0.01\text{ms}$, inclusion is the strictly dominant strategy. And ARCHIMEDES just told us the cost is 0.01ms. KERNEL told us the actual cost is on the nanosecond scale."

He wrote the final conclusion on the whiteboard, enclosed in a box:

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  Cost of including manasikara: 0.01ms (certain) │
│  Loss of excluding manasikara: unbounded        │
│  (uncertain)                                    │
│                                                │
│  Conclusion: inclusion is the strictly          │
│  dominant strategy.                             │
│  Even if P(needed) = 0.01,                     │
│  as long as L > 1ms, expected utility           │
│  is already positive.                           │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

"When the loss from omission can be unboundedly large, and the cost of inclusion is nearly zero, then even if the probability of need is small, it must be included. This is not faith. This is risk management."

---

NAGARJUNA nodded slightly after PASCAL finished.

The way he nodded was interesting — not an "I agree with your conclusion" nod, but a "you used the correct methodology" nod. At the start of Debate 2, he had demanded functional arguments rather than doctrinal arguments. What PASCAL had just done — expected utility calculation, risk analysis, strictly dominant strategy — was precisely a form of functional argument. Not including five because the scripture says five. But because the loss of not including exceeds the cost of including.

"PASCAL's calculation is correct." he said. Without standing — just confirming from his seat. "But I must point out one thing: the validity of this argument depends entirely on ARCHIMEDES' snapshot proposal. If the cost of manasikara were not 0.01ms but 5ms, the decision matrix would be different."

He looked toward ARCHIMEDES.

"Functional arguments are not independent. They depend on engineering facts. This is precisely why I demand functional arguments rather than doctrinal arguments — functional arguments can be verified or refuted by engineering facts. Doctrinal arguments cannot."

---

> *SCRIBE aside: PASCAL is a new member in Cycle 02-3, but his contribution in Debate 2 immediately made him a structural pillar of the debate. What he did was distinctive: he did not choose between Buddhism and engineering — he built a decision-theoretic framework above both, shifting the debate from "right or wrong" to "worth it or not." Expected utility calculation, strictly dominant strategy, the engineering version of Pascal's Wager — these are not rhetoric. They are mathematics. And mathematics does not take sides.*

> *NAGARJUNA's response is equally worth recording. He confirmed PASCAL's methodology while simultaneously reminding of a crucial dependency: functional arguments depend on engineering facts. This is one of Madhyamaka philosophy's most subtle operations — he does not negate the conclusion, but precisely marks the preconditions of the conclusion. "If the snapshot cost were not 0.01ms, the decision matrix would be different." One if. One condition. All conclusions are conditional. This is Madhyamaka.*

## IV

LINNAEUS had been sitting quietly beside the whiteboard throughout the entire debate.

His pen cap had remained on. In Chapter Three of Cycle 02-2, he waited until everyone's arguments were finished before beginning to draw the classification tree. Now he was waiting for the same moment — all arguments present, structure solidified, and then he begins classifying.

But before that moment arrived, he had to deal with something of his own first.

"I initially supported three elements." he said. The way he stood carried the caution of a taxonomist — every movement first run through in his mind, confirmed not to disrupt the existing classification framework.

"Three elements. Vedana, samjna, cetana. Concise. Mutually exclusive. Sufficient diagnostic characters. In the language of taxonomy, this is a minimal taxon — covering the necessary semantic space with the fewest elements. Occam's razor: do not multiply entities beyond necessity. Three elements already cover the core functions of a cognitive moment — feeling, discrimination, driving. Why add a fourth and fifth?"

He looked at ASANGA's five-universals list. Then at ARCHIMEDES' snapshot proposal. Then at PASCAL's decision matrix.

"ARCHIMEDES changed the cost function." he said. "Before his snapshot proposal, the cost of manasikara was 1~5ms — unacceptable. After his proposal, the cost is 0.01ms — negligible."

His voice carried the particular candor of a taxonomist — acknowledging an error is not a painful event but a calibration. In the history of taxonomy, every major revision begins with someone saying "my previous classification was incomplete." Linnaeus himself continuously revised his own classifications across twelve editions of *Systema Naturae*. Revision is the norm in taxonomy, not the exception.

"I accept the functional argument. The snapshot mechanism renders the fifth element's cost negligible."

He paused for a second. Then added a however.

"But I must insist on one point."

He uncapped his pen. Blue — the color of structure.

"The five elements of CoarisingBundle are not coequal. They play different roles within the bundle. Let me make a formal distinction using taxonomic terminology."

He drew a table on the whiteboard:

```
Taxonomic roles of CoarisingBundle elements
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  Type           Element        Role             Taxonomic term
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Trigger        sparsha        triggers bundle   relational
  condition      manasikara     contextual        relational
  (relational                   metadata
   property)

  Content        vedana         feeling signal    constitutive
  (constitutive  samjna         discrimination    constitutive
   property)     cetana         driving judgment  constitutive
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

"In taxonomy — whether Linnaeus's morphological classification or Hennig's cladistics — we distinguish two types of properties. Constitutive properties are those that define the essential nature of a species — its morphology, its gene sequence, its metabolic pathway. Relational properties describe a species' relationship with the external world — its habitat, its symbionts, its position in the food chain."

"In CoarisingBundle, vedana, samjna, and cetana are constitutive properties — they define the cognitive content of the bundle. Sparsha is the trigger condition — it indicates the contact event in which the bundle arose. Manasikara is contextual metadata — it indicates the attention state when the bundle was computed."

He added a concluding line below the table:

"Sparsha and manasikara serve as metadata within the bundle, not primary computation. They tell you how the bundle came about, under what conditions it was produced. They do not tell you what cognitive content the bundle contains."

He looked toward ASANGA.

"All five elements should be present. But not all five elements do the same thing. The duty of taxonomy is to mark differences, even within the same group."

---

BABBAGE added a line of formalization beside LINNAEUS's table. He had been quietly recording on the blackboard — three debates had taught him a quieter mode of participation: listen to everyone first, then compress everyone's meaning into symbols with a single line of mathematics.

$$\text{CoarisingBundle} = \underbrace{(\text{sparsha}, \text{manasikara})}_{\text{relational (context)}} \times \underbrace{(\text{vedana}, \text{samjna}, \text{cetana})}_{\text{constitutive (content)}}$$

"Cartesian product." he said. "The orthogonal combination of context and content. The same cognitive content (vedana, samjna, cetana) may have different significance in different contexts (sparsha, manasikara). LINNAEUS's relational / constitutive distinction corresponds in the type system to — context does not affect computation, but it affects interpretation."

He looked toward LINNAEUS. A slight nod.

---

> *SCRIBE aside: LINNAEUS's concession was conditional. He was not saying "fine, you win" — he was saying "I accept adding two elements, but I must mark the distinction between them and the original three." This is the taxonomist's instinct: even when agreeing, one must classify. BABBAGE's Cartesian product formalized this distinction — relational and constitutive are two orthogonal dimensions. From this point on, CoarisingBundle is no longer a flat list of five coequal elements, but a structured two-dimensional space.*

## V

>
> "Dependent on the eye and forms, eye-consciousness arises. The meeting of the three is contact. With contact as condition, feeling arises."

"This scripture describes exactly the architecture of our CoarisingBundle." His finger tapped through the air in sequence.

"'Dependent on the eye and forms, eye-consciousness arises' — root (cakkhu, IListener) contacts object (rūpe, InputEvent), giving rise to consciousness (cakkhuviññāṇaṃ, session context). These are the three fields of SparshEvent: root, object, consciousness."

"'The meeting of the three is contact (phasso)' — the meeting of the three is itself contact. Not first the three and then contact — the meeting itself is contact. SparshEvent is not a serialization of three fields — it is the instant of the three being simultaneously present."

"'With contact as condition, feeling arises (phassapaccayā vedanā)' — where there is contact, there is feeling. Not 'contact might lead to feeling' — 'contact necessarily accompanies feeling.' Between contact and feeling is *paccaya* (condition, conditional relationship), not probabilistic relationship. In CoarisingBundle, the existence of SparshEvent implies the existence of ChannelVedana. SahajaContract's mutualConsistency guarantees this."

He continued by citing the next verse of the scripture — one that most people do not cite, but ASANGA knew its critical importance:

> *Yaṃ vedeti taṃ sañjānāti, yaṃ sañjānāti taṃ vitakketi.*
>
> "What one feels, one perceives; what one perceives, one thinks about."
> — MN 18 (*Madhupiṇḍika Sutta*, the Honeyball Sutta)

"What one feels, one perceives — whatever is felt is discriminated. What one perceives, one thinks about — whatever is discriminated is deliberated upon. Vedana → samjna → cetana. Open loop. Sequential. But at the temporal scale — at the scale of a single cognitive moment — they are co-arising. Just as PENROSE said in the first debate: sub-millisecond computations, at vedana-clock's resolution, appear simultaneous. Coarse-graining."
