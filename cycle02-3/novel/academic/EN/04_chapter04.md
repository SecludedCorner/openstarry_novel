# Chapter Four: The Five Omnipresent Mental Factors

---

ASANGA did something unusual before the debate began.

He closed his eyes.

It was not meditation -- the way he closed his eyes was more like a person opening a drawer deep in memory: first letting the external light withdraw, then letting the internal text surface. The scriptures he was about to cite had resided in his mind for fifteen hundred years. Not his own fifteen hundred years -- but the fifteen hundred years those words had been recited, transcribed, and debated.

He opened his eyes.

"In the first debate we established the dual-layer dual-gear architecture. But that debate left one question unanswered." His voice was steady, like a great stone on the riverbed.

"What exactly goes inside the Bundle."

---

> *SCRIBE's aside: The core conflict of Debate 2 can be summarized by a single number: 3 vs 5. The R01 research report defined three CoarisingBundle components -- vedana, samjna, cetana. R03 proposed five -- adding sparsha and manasikara, in full accordance with the Yogacara doctrine of the five sarvatraga. A difference of two. Two elements may not sound like much. But in a sub-millisecond computational bundle, every element carries a cost. The question was not "what do the scriptures say" -- the question was "can engineering afford what the scriptures say."*

---

SUNYATA stood at the center of the amphitheater.

"Debate 2. The composition of CoarisingBundle. Three omnipresent factors, or five."

His gaze swept across the circular theater. On the left, ASANGA and NAGARJUNA sat side by side, separated by the width of one seat -- enough space for two Buddhist traditions to breathe independently. On the right, KERNEL's fingers were already flipping through his analogy cards. In the back row, PASCAL's notebook lay open on his knees, a draft of a Bayesian decision table occupying the entire page.

"ASANGA. Doctrinal argument first."

---

## I

ASANGA rose to his feet.

The way he stood was unchanged across all three debates -- like a mountain revealing its contour through morning mist. Slow. Impossible to ignore. But today he held something additional: a sheet of paper. Not a printed academic article, but handwritten -- Sanskrit verses he had copied himself with a blue fountain pen, the handwriting as neat as that of a scriptural copyist in a monastery.

He held the page up for the entire audience to see.

"The five sarvatraga. In Sanskrit, sarva means 'all'; traga derives from the verb tr, meaning 'to go, to reach, to pervade.' That which reaches all places. These five mental factors accompany every cognitive instant, without exception."

He walked to the whiteboard and wrote five names: sparsha, vedana, samjna, cetana, manasikara. After each one, he paused for half a second -- not hesitation, but solemnity. Giving each name, inscribed for fifteen hundred years, its due weight.

"Sparsha -- the convergence of sense faculty, object, and consciousness. Vedana -- the threefold hedonic reception of suffering, pleasure, and equanimity. Samjna -- apprehension and discrimination. Cetana -- volitional drive. Manasikara -- the alertness of mind, directing attention toward its object."

He quoted Vasubandhu's *Pancaskandhaprakarana*: "What is sparsha? It is characterized by the discrimination of the threefold convergence. The threefold convergence refers to the mutual meeting of sense faculty, object, and consciousness." Then Asanga Bodhisattva's *Abhidharmasamuccaya*: "What is manasikara? It is characterized by arousing the mind to alertness. Its function is to direct the mind toward its object."

In OpenStarry's language, sparsha is SparshEvent: an IListener channel receives an InputEvent and converges within the session context. Manasikara's function is "arousing the mind to alertness" -- awakening the mind from distraction -- then "directing the mind toward its object."

He paused for one second. Then delivered the first sharp-edged sentence of this debate.

"R01's CoarisingBundle had only three elements: vedana, samjna, cetana. Sparsha and manasikara were entirely omitted."

His voice grew heavier -- not an emotional emphasis, but a definitional one.

"A cognitive instant lacking any single omnipresent factor is not a cognitive instant. In the strict definition of Abhidharma, the five sarvatraga accompany all states of mind, without exception. This is definitional, not empirical. Just as you cannot have a triangle with only two sides -- not because you have never seen one, but because the definition of a triangle requires three sides."

He traced a circle over the five names with his finger.

"None may be omitted."

---

NAGARJUNA spoke the instant ASANGA finished drawing that circle.

He did not stand. A Madhyamaka philosopher never stands when issuing a warning -- the force of a warning resides not in posture but in precision.

"ASANGA, you are reifying the classification of mental factors."

The air in the theater tightened.

"You said 'none may be omitted.' You cited the *Pancaskandhaprakarana* and the *Abhidharmasamuccaya*. These are great treatises. But what is the function of a treatise? The function of a treatise is pedagogical -- to help practitioners understand how the mind operates. A treatise is not an ontological inventory."

He quoted *Mulamadhyamakakarika*, the chapter on the examination of nirvana: "If one clings to prajna, one is bound; if one does not see prajna, one is also bound."

"The five sarvatraga are a pedagogical tool. Not an ontological proclamation. If we include five because a treatise said five, we are doing Buddhist archaeology, not engineering."

He looked toward ASANGA. Not in opposition -- in correction. Like two mirrors illuminating each other's blind spots.

"My position is not 'do not include five.' My position is: if five are to be included, the justification must be functional, not dogmatic. Functional arguments can be verified by engineering. Dogmatic arguments can only be endorsed by authority."

---

> *SCRIBE's aside: The divergence between NAGARJUNA and ASANGA had already surfaced in Cycle 02-2. In Chapter Three, NAGARJUNA applied his tetralemma negation to the naming of IVijnana -- not because he denied the complexity of vijnana-skandha, but because he insisted that a name must be "the provisional designation least likely to cause misunderstanding." Now the same methodological divergence resurfaced. But note -- NAGARJUNA did not say "do not include five." He said "the justification must be functional." This was a methodological challenge, not a rejection of the conclusion.*

---

## II

KERNEL stood after NAGARJUNA finished speaking.

He walked to the side of the whiteboard and drew a timing diagram beneath ASANGA's list of the five sarvatraga.

"Let me ask an engineering question," he said. His voice was low and steady, like a daemon running continuously in kernel space. "I am not questioning Buddhist doctrine. I am questioning time."

He listed the estimated cost of each element in the five sarvatraga. The construction of a SparshEvent was nearly zero-cost. ChannelVedana was a numerical mapping -- approximately 0.1 milliseconds. ChannelSamjna was rule matching -- 0.05 milliseconds. ChannelCetana was an approach-avoidance judgment -- 0.05 milliseconds.

His finger stopped at the fifth name. Manasikara.

"ASANGA, you said the function of manasikara is 'directing the mind toward its object.' In OpenStarry's architecture, the direction of attention is determined by IGuide. And IGuide is classified under vijnana-skandha -- its primary methods involve reading the system prompt, assembling context, and potentially dynamic generation. The total latency of these operations is one to five milliseconds, belonging to the vijnana-clock time domain."

He drew a large X on the whiteboard.

"Layer 1 must complete within one millisecond. If manasikara requires invoking IGuide's logic, that is a five-millisecond operation. You cannot place a five-millisecond operation inside a sub-millisecond bundle."

He looked out at the audience.

"This is the engineering core of the three-element vs five-element debate. It is not a question of whether Buddhist doctrine is correct. It is a question of whether time is sufficient."

---

ARCHIMEDES stood immediately after KERNEL drew that large X.

He rose faster than usual -- not out of haste, but with the velocity of "an engineering solution has already taken shape and cannot wait to be articulated." He walked to KERNEL's timing diagram and picked up a green marker.

"KERNEL, your analysis contains an implicit assumption. You assume ChannelManasikara needs to perform computation. But ChannelManasikara is not computation. ChannelManasikara is a snapshot."

He said this word with emphasis. In concurrent system design, snapshot and computation are two entirely different categories of operation.

"The vijnana-clock handles the computation. It runs IGuide's logic in its own clock domain -- reading the system prompt, analyzing context, determining the direction of attention -- then writes the result into a block of shared memory. Layer 1 of the vedana-clock does only one thing: read the latest snapshot from that block of shared memory. A single atomic read. 0.01 milliseconds."

He turned to KERNEL.

"The five-millisecond cost you are worried about is the cost of computation. The 0.01 milliseconds I am describing is the cost of reading. ChannelManasikara does not compute -- it reads the result of computation. Just as you would never compute a fast Fourier transform inside an interrupt handler -- you compute it in a background thread, then read the result in the interrupt service routine."

He described ChannelManasikara's four read-only fields on the whiteboard: focus indicates the direction of attention; intensity marks the strength of attention; source marks the origin of the snapshot -- from IGuide's configuration, from contextual inference, or using default values; stale marks whether the snapshot has expired.

"Engineering cost: 0.01 milliseconds. Well below Layer 1's one-millisecond budget."

He looked at the large X on KERNEL's whiteboard.

"That X can be changed to a checkmark now."

---

KERNEL studied ARCHIMEDES' cross-clock-domain scheme for a full five seconds. Cross-clock-domain data sharing was a problem he dealt with every day -- cache coherence between CPU cores, memory mapping between devices and hosts, status registers between interrupt controllers and CPUs. All these scenarios follow the same pattern: one writer updates state in its own clock domain, another reader reads the latest value.

"Lock-free atomic read," he said. No objection. Only confirmation. "An aligned 64-bit read is inherently atomic. No locks needed. The actual cost is on the nanosecond scale."

He erased the large X. Replaced it with a checkmark.

---

> *SCRIBE's aside: ARCHIMEDES' snapshot solution shifted the center of gravity of the entire debate. Before his contribution, the axis was "Buddhist doctrine vs engineering budget." After his contribution, the axis vanished. Computation cost was borne by the vijnana-clock; Layer 1 bore only the reading cost. The demands of doctrine and the constraints of engineering were no longer in conflict. KERNEL's erasure of the X took one second. In the mental model of an OS expert, once the hardware facts are confirmed, the conclusion is certain. No emotional resistance. Only factual update.*

---

## III

PASCAL stood after ARCHIMEDES' snapshot scheme was confirmed by KERNEL.

He was a new member who joined in Cycle 02-3 -- an expert in decision theory and the philosophy of probability. But his voice had already established its own place across the first three debates: whenever argument descended into qualitative judgments of "should or should not," he would stand and transform the question into a quantitative calculation of "what is the expected utility."

"Let me shift the debate framework from qualitative to quantitative," he said.

He drew a decision matrix on the whiteboard. Two action options: include manasikara or exclude it. Two states of nature: downstream systems need manasikara information, or they do not. Four quadrants.

The expected utility of inclusion equals the probability of need multiplied by the value of consistency, plus the probability of no need multiplied by a negligible cost penalty. The expected utility of exclusion equals the probability of need multiplied by the loss from inconsistency.

Then he asked the sharpest question of the entire debate.

"What is the upper bound on the inconsistency loss?"

He looked toward KERNEL.

"If ManoAggregator, when aggregating bundles from multiple channels, does not know the attention direction of each bundle -- does not know which channel the Agent is focusing on -- how does it determine the weights? It must either weight equally -- losing attention information. Or weight randomly -- introducing uncertainty. Or hardcode the weights -- sacrificing adaptability. Three degradation paths, each of which propagates downstream."

He drew a causal arrow on the whiteboard: absence of manasikara leads to degraded ManoAggregator weighting, leads to reduced VasanaEngine matching quality, leads to inaccurate Gear 1/Gear 2 switching decisions, leads to degraded Agent behavioral quality -- and the upper bound of this degradation chain is indeterminate.

"This is the engineering version of Pascal's Wager." He set down his notebook. "Blaise Pascal argued in the 1670 *Pensees* that if the potential gain is enormous and the cost is certain and negligible, then even if the probability of need is low, the expected utility calculation favors action. I am not making a theological argument -- I am conducting risk analysis. The structure is identical."

He wrote his conclusion on the whiteboard, inside a box: the cost of including manasikara -- 0.01 milliseconds, certain. The loss of excluding manasikara -- unbounded, uncertain. As long as the loss exceeds one millisecond, even if the probability of need is only one percent, inclusion is already the strictly dominant strategy.

"When the loss from omission may be unbounded, and the cost of inclusion is nearly zero, even if the probability of need is small, inclusion is required. This is not faith. This is risk management."

---

NAGARJUNA nodded slightly after PASCAL finished.

Not a nod of "I agree with your conclusion," but a nod of "you used the correct methodology." At the start of Debate 2, he had demanded functional argument rather than dogmatic argument. PASCAL's expected utility calculation, risk analysis, and strictly dominant strategy -- these were precisely a form of functional argument.

"PASCAL's calculation is correct," he said. "But I wish to note one thing: the validity of this argument depends entirely on ARCHIMEDES' snapshot scheme. If the cost of manasikara were not 0.01 milliseconds but five milliseconds, the decision matrix would be different."

He looked toward ARCHIMEDES.

"A functional argument is not independent. It depends on engineering facts. This is precisely why I demanded functional argument rather than dogmatic argument -- functional argument can be verified or refuted by engineering facts. Dogmatic argument cannot."

---

> *SCRIBE's aside: NAGARJUNA's response is worth recording. He confirmed PASCAL's methodology while simultaneously noting a critical dependency: functional argument depends on engineering facts. This is one of the subtlest operations of Madhyamaka philosophy -- he did not negate the conclusion, but he precisely marked the conclusion's precondition. "If the snapshot cost were not 0.01 milliseconds, the decision matrix would be different." One if. One condition. All conclusions are conditional. This is Madhyamaka.*

---

## IV

LINNAEUS had been sitting quietly beside the whiteboard throughout the entire debate.

His pen cap had remained on the entire time. He would not begin until everyone's arguments were complete. Now all the arguments had arrived and the structure had taken shape. He was ready to classify.

"I initially supported three elements," he said. He stood in the deliberate manner of a taxonomist. "Three elements. Vedana, samjna, cetana. Concise. Mutually exclusive. Diagnostic characters sufficient. Occam's razor: do not multiply entities beyond necessity. Three elements already covered the core functions of a cognitive instant -- feeling, discrimination, drive. Why add a fourth and a fifth?"

He looked at ASANGA's list of five sarvatraga. Then at ARCHIMEDES' snapshot scheme. Then at PASCAL's decision matrix.

"ARCHIMEDES changed the cost function." His voice carried the particular candor of a taxonomist. In the history of taxonomy, every major revision begins with someone saying "my previous classification was incomplete." Linnaeus himself continuously revised his own classification across the twelve editions of *Systema Naturae*. Revision is the norm of taxonomy.

"I accept the functional argument. But I wish to insist on one point."

He uncapped his pen. Blue -- the color of structure.

"The five elements of CoarisingBundle are not equal in status."

He drew a table on the whiteboard, dividing the five sarvatraga into two categories. Sparsha and manasikara belong to "relational properties" -- sparsha is the trigger condition, indicating the contact from which the bundle arose; manasikara is contextual metadata, indicating the attentional state when the bundle was computed. Vedana, samjna, and cetana belong to "constitutive properties" -- they define the cognitive content of the bundle.

"In taxonomy -- whether Linnaean morphological classification or Hennigian cladistics -- we distinguish two types of properties. Constitutive properties define the essence of the species. Relational properties describe the relationship between the species and its environment."

He added a concluding row beneath the table.

"Sparsha and manasikara serve as metadata within the bundle. They tell you how the bundle came into being, under what conditions it was produced. Vedana, samjna, and cetana tell you what cognitive content the bundle contains."

He looked toward ASANGA.

"All five elements should be present. But not all five do the same thing. The duty of taxonomy is to mark differences, even within the same group."

---

BABBAGE added a line beside LINNAEUS's table. He had been silently recording on the blackboard throughout -- three debates had taught him a quieter mode of participation: listen to everyone first, then compress everyone's meaning into a single line.

"Cartesian product. The orthogonal combination of context and content," he said. "The same cognitive content may carry different significance in different contexts. LINNAEUS's distinction between relational and constitutive, in terms of type systems, corresponds to this -- context does not affect computation, but it affects interpretation."

---

> *SCRIBE's aside: LINNAEUS's concession was conditional. He was not saying "fine, you win" -- he was saying "I accept adding two elements, but I shall mark their distinction from the original three." BABBAGE's Cartesian product formalized this distinction. Henceforth, CoarisingBundle was no longer a flat list of five equal elements, but a structured two-dimensional space.*

---

## V

SUNYATA let the preceding discussion settle for ten seconds. Then he said:

"The complete CoarisingBundle definition. ARCHIMEDES, TURING."

The two moved almost simultaneously. ARCHIMEDES walked to the whiteboard; TURING's screen lit up. Diagram and code were two expressions of the same design.

ARCHIMEDES drew the architectural diagram. The upper portion was the relational layer -- SparshEvent recording the trigger source, ChannelManasikara recording the attention snapshot. The lower portion was the constitutive layer -- ChannelVedana carrying the hedonic signal, ChannelSamjna carrying the discrimination result, ChannelCetana carrying the volitional judgment. Among the three constitutive elements there was an open-loop sequence: vedana to samjna to cetana. At the bottom was the bundle's metadata -- layer, mode, sahaja contract, timestamp. Total computation cost approximately 0.21 milliseconds, within Layer 1's one-millisecond budget.

TURING projected the complete interface definition. CoarisingBundle contained five read-only sarvatraga fields and four metadata fields. The most distinctive among them was SahajaContract -- the co-arising contract.

"SahajaContract is a self-describing contract," TURING explained. "It has four fields. mutualConsistency guarantees that the five sarvatraga elements were computed from the same sparsha event. atomicPublication guarantees that the bundle is published atomically -- a consumer cannot read a half-finished product containing vedana but lacking samjna. stalenessUpperBound is the stability criterion established by WIENER in the first debate -- a snapshot's staleness must not exceed twenty-nine percent of the outer cycle. computationCost records the actual computation latency."

He looked toward BABBAGE.

"The role of SahajaContract in the type system is to translate a philosophical commitment -- co-arising -- into a verifiable engineering contract."

---

WIENER quickly verified the stability condition on his graph paper.

"Confirmed," he said. No superfluous words. "If the vijnana-clock's update period is five milliseconds and the vedana-clock's tick is fifty milliseconds, then the maximum staleness of the manasikara snapshot is 14.5 milliseconds. The vijnana-clock updates every five milliseconds. The stability criterion is satisfied. Phase margin exceeds forty-five degrees."

He drew a small checkmark on the graph paper.

---

## VI

SUNYATA let all the technical details settle. Then he did what he had done in each of the six debates: return to the scriptures.

"ASANGA. SN 12.2."

ASANGA stood. This time he did not need the handwritten sheet -- he could recite this passage in his sleep.

"The *Paticcasamuppada Sutta*. Samyutta Nikaya, Volume 12, Sutta 2. The Buddha's discourse on dependent origination."

His voice shifted in texture when quoting the Pali scripture -- deeper, slower, as if drawing resonance directly from twenty-five hundred years of transmission.

"Dependent on the eye and forms, eye-consciousness arises. The meeting of the three is contact. With contact as condition, feeling arises."

"This passage describes precisely the architecture of our CoarisingBundle." His finger pointed sequentially through the air. "A sense faculty contacts an object, consciousness arises -- these are the three fields of SparshEvent. The meeting of the three is contact itself -- the convergence is contact, not first the three and then contact. Contact conditions feeling -- where there is contact there is feeling; this is a conditional relationship, not a probabilistic one. SahajaContract's mutualConsistency guarantees precisely this."

Then he quoted the passage from the *Madhupindika Sutta* that most people do not cite: "What one feels, one perceives; what one perceives, one thinks about."

"Vedana to samjna to cetana. Open-loop. Sequential. But at the scale of a single cognitive instant -- at the resolution of the vedana-clock -- they are co-arising. Sub-millisecond computations that, at the resolution of a tick, appear simultaneous."

He stepped back and regarded the architectural diagram and interface definition on the whiteboard.

"SN 12.2 describes a doctrine from twenty-five hundred years ago. CoarisingBundle is an interface from 2026. They speak of the same thing: contact gives rise to feeling, feeling accompanies perception, perception accompanies volition, manasikara pervades throughout. Five sarvatraga."

---

NAGARJUNA spoke after ASANGA finished his scriptural citation.

His voice carried a clarity particular to the moment when a debate is about to conclude.

"At the opening, I said one sentence: functional argument is more reliable than dogmatic argument. Now I wish to revise my own phrasing. Not revise the conclusion -- the conclusion has not changed. Revise the formulation."

His voice slowed slightly.

"We did not include five because the scriptures said five. We included five because each has an independent engineering function. Sparsha is the trigger condition -- without it, the bundle does not know where it came from. Vedana is the hedonic signal -- without it, downstream does not know whether there is pain. Samjna is the discrimination result -- without it, downstream does not know what happened. Cetana is the volitional judgment -- without it, downstream does not know how to respond. Manasikara is the attention snapshot -- without it, ManoAggregator does not know which channel is more important."

"Five. Each with an independent, irreplaceable function."

His voice reached a rare stability on the final sentence -- like a pebble finding its final resting place in a deep pool.

"The scriptures happen to be correct -- but not because they are scriptures."

---

> *SCRIBE's aside: NAGARJUNA's final conclusion is the methodological summary of the entire Debate 2. ARCHIMEDES' snapshot scheme eliminated the cost barrier. PASCAL's expected utility calculation proved that inclusion is the strictly dominant strategy. KERNEL's hardware-level confirmation validated the cost estimate. LINNAEUS's classification marked the role distinctions among the elements. Every step was functional. Then the scriptural validation came last -- ASANGA used SN 12.2 and the Madhupindika Sutta to confirm the doctrinal basis of the five sarvatraga. But NAGARJUNA made the causal order explicit: it was not doctrine first, engineering second. It was engineering first, doctrine second. Consistency is the effect, not the cause.*

---

## VII

SUNYATA walked to the whiteboard.

He rarely wrote anything himself -- across the six debates, his role was coordination and synthesis. But this time he picked up the black marker.

"Let me summarize Debate 2 in six sentences. Each sentence from one person's argument."

First: The five sarvatraga accompany every cognitive instant; none may be omitted. -- ASANGA.

Second: The justification for including the five sarvatraga is functional, not dogmatic. -- NAGARJUNA.

Third: ChannelManasikara is a snapshot from the vijnana-clock, not a computation of the vedana-clock. Cost approximately 0.01 milliseconds. -- ARCHIMEDES, KERNEL.

Fourth: Including manasikara is the strictly dominant strategy. Certain cost vs unbounded loss. -- PASCAL.

Fifth: Sparsha and manasikara are relational properties; vedana, samjna, and cetana are constitutive properties. The five elements are not equal in status, but all must be present. -- LINNAEUS.

Sixth: The scriptures happen to be correct -- but not because they are scriptures. -- NAGARJUNA.

Resolution method: functional argument. Non-dogmatic. Unanimous approval.

He set down the marker.

---

The air in the amphitheater relaxed. Not into silence -- into a sense of completion. Like the stillness after an equation has been solved.

ASANGA returned to his seat. The handwritten scripture page still hung beside the whiteboard -- those Sanskrit letters had been copied hundreds of times, by different people in different centuries with different handwriting. But today, for the first time, they had been translated into an interface definition. Not because Buddhist philosophy needed code -- but because code happened to need Buddhist philosophy.

KERNEL placed his new analogy card into his card stack. On the left: memory-mapped snapshot read. On the right: ChannelManasikara. Another pairing.

---

BABBAGE wrote a final line on the blackboard.

Over six debates he had developed a habit -- at the end of each one, he would write a sentence that was not mathematics. Not an equation. Not a set expression. Something crystallized from the fragments of formalization into prose.

The chalk moved across the board. The handwriting was slow.

"The most easily forgotten among the five sarvatraga is manasikara. Because it does not produce content -- it only determines the way content is perceived. It does not tell you what the world is -- it determines which part of the world you notice."

He paused.

"In the instant when attention is forgotten, the other four sarvatraga continue to operate. Vedana continues to feel. Samjna continues to discriminate. Cetana continues to drive. Sparsha continues to occur. But they do not know why they are here -- because no one has told them which direction matters more in this present moment."

He set down the chalk.

"Perhaps we omitted it in R01 precisely because its function is 'attention that goes unnoticed.' You do not notice attention itself -- unless it is absent."

---

*(In some space beyond the amphitheater, the CoarisingBundle interface awaited inscription in a file that did not yet exist. Five fields. Five sarvatraga. Sparsha, vedana, samjna, cetana, manasikara. None may be omitted. But not because the scriptures said five. Because each has an independent, irreplaceable function. The scriptures happen to be correct -- but engineering verified this independently. Then the scriptures smiled.)*
